Urban multidimensional spatial coupling coordination and carbon emissions in China: Mechanisms and nonlinear effectsAbstractCities are increasingly governed through digital networks as well as physical infrastructure, but the environmental implications of this coordination are not automatically positive. This study examines 284 Chinese prefecture-level and above cities from 2006 to 2024 by incorporating digital space into the production-living-ecological urban functional framework. The results show an inverted U-shaped relationship between multidimensional spatial coordination and log-transformed carbon emissions, with a turning point of 0.542. The pattern reflects a coordination paradox: integration can reduce information and allocation frictions, yet early-stage coordination may also intensify infrastructure expansion, industrial reorganization, and energy demand. Most sample cities remain below the turning point, indicating that the efficiency-oriented stage is not yet dominant. Evidence from restructuring equations suggests that industrial upgrading and green innovation are stage-dependent pathways rather than immediate emission-reduction pathways. Digital economy development mainly reduces the marginal emission cost associated with coordination and flattens the nonlinear curvature. Supplementary instrumental-variable evidence is consistent with the baseline pattern but is interpreted cautiously. The findings suggest that digitally enabled coordination can support sustainable urban transition only when data-enabled governance shifts cities from expansion-oriented integration toward efficiency-oriented operation.Keywords: Urban spatial coordination; Digital space; Carbon emissions; Industrial restructuring; Green innovation; Data-enabled governance1. IntroductionCities concentrate population, infrastructure, production, energy use, and emissions, making them the critical arena in which carbon mitigation and sustainable transition are ultimately realized (Seto et al., 2012; Dhakal, 2009; Creutzig et al., 2016). In China, rapid urbanization has unfolded alongside infrastructure expansion, industrial restructuring, and an accelerating digital transition, together reshaping the spatial organization of cities while intensifying pressures on resources and the environment (Fang et al., 2015; Yang et al., 2022). Against the dual-carbon agenda, understanding whether and how urban spatial reorganization can curb emissions has therefore become a central question for both urban governance and sustainability research.Existing research has mainly examined carbon emissions from the perspectives of urban scale, density, compactness, land expansion, and spatial structure. A large body of work shows that urban form influences commuting demand, infrastructure efficiency, and energy consumption, but the direction and magnitude of the effect vary across contexts (Gaigné et al., 2012; Lee and Lee, 2014; Kaza, 2020). Evidence from China and other countries further suggests that urban expansion, land-use restructuring, and agglomeration may either increase emissions through construction booms and transport demand or reduce them through density economies and efficiency gains (Castells-Quintana et al., 2021; Hong et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024).At the same time, digitalization is reshaping the spatial logic of urban development. Digital infrastructure, platform connectivity, data interaction, and intelligent governance increasingly shape the organization of production, living, and ecological activities. Digital space is therefore not merely an external technological condition. It functions as an urban spatial dimension because it structures flows of information, services, capital, logistics, and governance across physical places (Balogun et al., 2020; Anon Higon et al., 2017). Yet digitalization also brings electricity use, equipment replacement, internet-related energy demand, and rebound effects, so its environmental consequences remain contested (Lange et al., 2020; Obringer et al., 2021).This creates a central tension for sustainable-city research. Spatial coordination can reduce coordination frictions, improve resource allocation, and support cleaner urban operation. At the same time, it can intensify infrastructure expansion, functional reorganization, industrial agglomeration, and energy demand. This coordination paradox is especially relevant in rapidly transforming cities, where governance systems must decide whether coordination remains expansion-oriented or becomes efficiency-oriented.Despite these advances, three gaps remain. First, most studies still treat urban space as a predominantly physical system and rarely incorporate digital space into the analytical framework of production, living, and ecological functions. Second, the literature has paid more attention to isolated dimensions of urban form than to coupling coordination among multiple urban functional spaces, even though recent functional-zone evidence shows that intra-urban functional heterogeneity matters for emission outcomes (Tan et al., 2024). Third, the restructuring pathways connecting multidimensional coordination and emissions remain insufficiently theorized, particularly when industrial upgrading and green innovation create short-run transition costs.Accordingly, this study investigates how urban multidimensional spatial coupling coordination affects carbon emissions in China under digital transformation. It extends the conventional production-living-ecological framework by treating digital space as a fourth functional dimension and constructs a spatial coupling coordination degree (SCCD) index covering production, living, ecological, and digital functions. The main empirical sample covers 284 prefecture-level and above cities during 2006-2024.The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews the relevant literature. Section 3 discusses the mechanisms through which urban multidimensional spatial coupling coordination affects carbon emissions and develops the hypotheses. Section 4 presents the data, variables, and model specification. Section 5 reports the empirical results. Section 6 concludes and discusses policy implications.2. Literature review2.1 Urban spatial restructuring and carbon emissionsUrban spatial restructuring is widely regarded as a key determinant of urban environmental performance. Existing studies show that urban expansion, land-use change, infrastructure investment, and the reorganization of production and residential activities reshape energy demand and carbon emissions (Seto et al., 2012; Fang et al., 2015; Castells-Quintana et al., 2021; Hong et al., 2022). One strand argues that compact development can lower per-capita emissions through density economies, shorter travel distances, and more efficient infrastructure (Gaigné et al., 2012; Lee and Lee, 2014; Kaza, 2020). Another emphasizes that rapid spatial expansion and functional reconfiguration often entail construction booms, additional transport demand, and higher energy use, especially in developing and industrializing contexts (Dhakal, 2009; Castells-Quintana et al., 2021). Recent functional-zone evidence further shows that the carbon implications of urban form depend on the spatial heterogeneity of land-use and transport functions within cities (Tan et al., 2024).Recent research has extended this debate to urban spatial form, especially monocentric and polycentric structures. Polycentric development may relieve congestion and reduce commuting-related emissions, but its carbon effect depends on population size, jobs-housing balance, inter-center connectivity, and regional development stage (Jung et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). Overall, the literature provides important insights into the environmental implications of urban morphology, yet it still focuses mainly on physical form rather than the coordinated operation of multiple urban functional dimensions.2.2 Digital space and multidimensional spatial coupling coordinationDigital technologies are reshaping the logic of urban development. Existing studies show that digitalization can support climate adaptation, resource optimization, and smarter urban governance (Balogun et al., 2020). Research on ICTs further suggests that digital tools can improve coordination efficiency and governance capacity, while also increasing electricity consumption, equipment demand, and rebound effects (Anon Higon et al., 2017; Lange et al., 2020; Obringer et al., 2021). For China, recent evidence indicates that digital city construction and the digital economy can affect emission performance through efficiency gains, industrial upgrading, technological innovation, and spatial spillovers (Yang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2023; Hou et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024).However, digital factors are usually modeled as external drivers rather than as an intrinsic spatial dimension. This is increasingly restrictive. Digital infrastructure, platform connectivity, data interaction, and intelligent governance shape where production is organized, how residents access services, how ecological monitoring is performed, and how public decisions are coordinated. Digital space is therefore functionally comparable to traditional urban spatial dimensions because it organizes flows and interactions across physical space.2.3 Research gaps and analytical positioningThis study makes three related contributions. Conceptually, it endogenizes digital space within the production-living-ecological urban functional framework, rather than treating digitalization only as an external policy shock or technological driver. Digital infrastructure, platform connectivity, data interaction, and intelligent governance are spatially consequential because they organize how urban functions connect, circulate, and respond. This framing strengthens the conceptual legitimacy of digital space as a functional urban dimension.Empirically, the study provides city-level evidence on the nonlinear relationship between multidimensional coordination and emissions using a unified 2006-2024 panel of 284 Chinese cities, with an inverted-U endpoint test and turning-point calculation. It also separates restructuring-equation evidence from moderation evidence. Industrial upgrading and green innovation are interpreted as transition-related pathways, digital economy development is the supported boundary condition, and polycentricity is retained only as a supplementary interaction check.3. It summarizes the direct nonlinear association between multidimensional coordination and lnCE, the transition-related restructuring pathways represented by industrial upgrading and green innovation, and the primary moderating role of digital economy development. Polycentric urban structure is retained as a supplementary interaction check rather than as a supported moderator.Fig. 1. Research framework and mechanism analysis diagram.3.1 Direct impact of urban multidimensional spatial coupling coordination on carbon emissionsUrban multidimensional coordination may affect emissions through both expansion effects and efficiency effects. At low coordination levels, cities often rely on infrastructure expansion, industrial concentration, and additional energy input to connect production, living, ecological, and digital functions. In this stage, the emission cost of construction, transport, and functional reorganization may dominate the benefits of smoother coordination (Dhakal, 2009; Fang et al., 2015; Hong et al., 2022).As coordination deepens, factor allocation can become more efficient, information frictions can decline, and data-enabled governance can improve urban operation. Better matching among land use, industrial activity, public services, ecological monitoring, and digital networks may reduce redundant investment and lower emissions per unit of urban output (Creutzig et al., 2016; Yang et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2023). The expected relationship is therefore nonlinear. It reflects a governance tradeoff between expansion-oriented integration and efficiency-oriented coordination.H1. Urban multidimensional spatial coupling coordination exerts an inverted U-shaped effect on carbon emissions.3.2 Indirect impact through industrial upgradingIndustrial upgrading is a transition-related restructuring pathway through which multidimensional coordination may be associated with emissions. Structural change alters sectoral composition, energy intensity, and production efficiency. Existing evidence shows that industrial structure change is closely related to regional emissions, while the digital economy can accelerate the shift toward more advanced sectors (Tian et al., 2019; Chang et al., 2023). As production, living, ecological, and digital spaces become more coordinated, better factor allocation and stronger market matching can support restructuring.The short-run implication is not necessarily low-carbon. During transition periods, emerging industries often coexist with traditional sectors. Restructuring requires new investment, equipment renewal, construction of modern facilities, and additional energy input. Multidimensional coordination may therefore be associated with industrial upgrading as a stage-dependent pathway, while the emission implication depends on the timing and quality of restructuring.H2. Industrial upgrading may operate as a transition-related restructuring pathway associated with the emission implications of urban multidimensional spatial coupling coordination.3.3 Indirect impact through green technological innovationGreen technological innovation is another stage-dependent pathway. Patent-based evidence shows that green innovation can reduce emissions, although the timing and magnitude vary across contexts (Du et al., 2019; Lin and Ma, 2022). A more coordinated urban system can strengthen knowledge spillovers, broaden application scenarios, and improve institutional conditions for cleaner production. Digital space matters because platforms, data resources, and intelligent systems can accelerate the generation, diffusion, and commercialization of green technologies.These dynamics can also raise short-run emissions. Research and development, experimentation, equipment replacement, commercialization, and technology adoption all require capital and energy. Efficiency gains may also be partly offset by rebound effects (Lange et al., 2020; Obringer et al., 2021). Green innovation is therefore best understood here as a stage-dependent pathway rather than an immediate emission-reduction pathway.H3. Green technological innovation may operate as a stage-dependent pathway associated with the emission implications of urban multidimensional spatial coupling coordination.3.4 Moderating role of digital economy developmentDigital economy development may condition the nonlinear relationship between multidimensional coordination and lnCE. At early coordination stages, integration across production, living, ecological, and digital functions can create expansion-related emission costs through new infrastructure, spatial reorganization, and intensified activity. A stronger digital economy can reduce these costs by improving information matching, logistics scheduling, public-service allocation, smart transport, energy management, and cross-departmental governance.The theoretical expectation is attenuation rather than an earlier turning point. Digital capability can reduce the marginal emission cost associated with coordination and flatten the nonlinear curvature. In governance terms, it can help shift coordination from a construction- and expansion-oriented process toward a data-enabled efficiency process. Accordingly, digital economy development is treated as the main boundary condition.H4. Digital economy development reshapes and attenuates the nonlinear emission implications of multidimensional spatial coupling coordination.3.5 Supplementary boundary condition: polycentric urban structureUrban spatial form provides another possible boundary condition. Although polycentric development is often associated with lower congestion and more balanced growth, its environmental implication is not uniformly positive (Jung et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). When centers are weakly connected or jobs and residences remain mismatched, polycentricity can raise coordination costs, lengthen commuting and logistics chains, and require repeated infrastructure investment. The revised manuscript therefore treats polycentricity as a supplementary interaction check rather than as a supported moderator.4. Data, variables and empirical strategy4.1 Data source and sampleThis study uses panel data for 284 prefecture-level and above cities in China over the period 2006-2024 as the main empirical sample, covering 5,396 city-year observations. The 2022-2024 observations are also derived from real statistical records or database sources rather than model-generated estimates. The sample window is determined by the availability of multidimensional spatial indicators and city-level carbon-emission data. The raw data are drawn mainly from the China City Statistical Yearbook, China Energy Statistical Yearbook, China Urban Construction Statistical Yearbook, China Rural Statistical Yearbook, China Industrial Statistical Yearbook, and China Environmental Statistical Yearbook. City-level carbon emissions are taken from the Emissions Database for Global Atmospheric Research (EDGAR), which ensures temporal comparability under a consistent accounting framework.4.2 Variables and index constructionThe dependent variable is lnCE, defined as ln(CE_raw + 1), where CE_raw denotes city-level carbon emissions. In the available Stata file, this log-transformed variable is stored under the original variable name CE; therefore, the empirical workflow renames CE as lnCE without applying an additional logarithmic transformation.The core explanatory variable is the spatial coupling coordination degree (SCCD) of urban multidimensional spatial functions. The indicator system is adapted to the production-living-ecological-digital framework emphasized in this study. It covers four functional dimensions, 13 criterion-level dimensions, and 36 specific indicators after deleting one policy-term frequency indicator. Larger SCCD values indicate stronger coordination across production, living, ecological, and digital spaces.Specifically, production space is captured by indicators reflecting agricultural, industrial, and service-sector output and efficiency; living space is measured by indicators of basic conditions, medical services, education, science and technology, and cultural resources; ecological space is measured by indicators of environmental pressure, environmental status, and environmental response; and digital space is measured by indicators of informatization, digital-intelligent development, and networked development.The SCCD index is constructed in six steps. First, each indicator is normalized according to its direction. For positive indicators, x_norm[j,it] = (x[j,it] - min(x[j])) / (max(x[j]) - min(x[j])). For negative indicators, x_norm[j,it] = (max(x[j]) - x[j,it]) / (max(x[j]) - min(x[j])).Second, the entropy-weighting method is applied within each functional subsystem. The normalized share is p[j,it] = x_norm[j,it] / sum_i sum_t x_norm[j,it]. The entropy value is e[j] = -k * sum_i sum_t p[j,it] ln(p[j,it]), and the indicator weight is w[j] = (1 - e[j]) / sum_j(1 - e[j]).Third, the development score of each functional space is calculated as U[k,it] = sum_j w[j] x_norm[j,it], where k denotes production, living, ecological, or digital space. Fourth, the comprehensive development index is T[it] = sum_k a[k] U[k,it], with equal weights a[k] = 1/4 assigned to the four functional spaces.Fifth, the coupling degree is C[it] = {prod_k U[k,it] / [(sum_k U[k,it] / 4)^4]}^(1/4). Finally, the coordination degree is SCCD[it] = sqrt(C[it] * T[it]). Equal weights across production, living, ecological, and digital spaces are used in the final coordination-degree construction to avoid allowing any single dimension to dominate the overall index. To capture the possibility that the emission implication changes across development stages, both SCCD and its squared term are included. H3 therefore receives mechanism-equation support as associated pathway evidence, not as decomposed pathway confirmation.Taken together, the mechanism-equation results suggest that multidimensional coordination is associated with restructuring processes that unfold over time. Industrial upgrading and green innovation may improve long-run efficiency, but they can also raise short-run energy demand. The governance challenge is to shorten the adjustment period and reduce rebound effects during structural transformation.5.5 Moderation and supplementary interaction checksTable 7 reports moderation and supplementary interaction checks for the 2006-2024 main sample. Conclusions and policy implications6.1 ConclusionsThis study investigates how urban multidimensional spatial coupling coordination is associated with emissions in China under digital transformation. By extending the conventional production-living-ecological framework to include digital space and by using panel data for 284 prefecture-level and above cities over 2006-2024, the paper identifies a coordination paradox. Multidimensional coordination can reduce frictions and improve allocation, but it can also intensify infrastructure expansion, industrial reorganization, and energy demand. Its environmental consequences therefore depend on transition stage and governance capacity.Three conclusions are central. First, SCCD exhibits a significant inverted U-shaped relationship with lnCE, with a turning point of 0.542. Because most cities remain below that point, the efficiency-oriented stage is not yet dominant for most of the sample. Second, the nonlinear pattern is robust to winsorization, the NTL urban-activity check, and supplementary IV estimation, although the IV results are used cautiously. Third, industrial upgrading and green innovation receive mechanism-equation support as stage-dependent restructuring pathways, while digital economy development is the supported boundary condition. POLY remains only a supplementary interaction check.Overall, the evidence does not imply that coordination automatically produces low-emission outcomes. The policy issue is governance sequencing. Cities below the turning point should avoid converting coordination into duplicated infrastructure and energy-intensive growth. Cities approaching the turning point should strengthen data-enabled governance, improve restructuring quality, and manage rebound risks. Cities already beyond the turning point should consolidate efficiency gains through institutional coordination, cross-sector data integration, and long-term monitoring.6.2 Policy implicationsFirst, urban low-carbon policy should prioritize governance quality rather than the simple expansion of coordinated systems. Because many sample cities remain on the left side of the turning point, raising formal coordination indicators is insufficient. Policy should focus on interoperable data systems, digital public services, and intelligent governance tools that reduce the marginal emission cost of coordination across production, living, ecological, and digital spaces.Second, policy design should manage the transition costs associated with industrial upgrading and green innovation. These restructuring processes do not automatically reduce emissions in the short run. Cleaner energy substitution, green finance, performance-based evaluation, and tighter coordination between innovation policy and industrial policy are needed to prevent transition costs from becoming persistent emission burdens.Third, cities pursuing polycentric development should pay closer attention to cross-center coordination. The current interaction estimates do not provide robust support for polycentricity as a moderator, but the theoretical risk remains clear. Dispersed centers can raise logistics costs, duplicate infrastructure, and weaken jobs-housing balance when cross-center links are incomplete. Polycentric planning should therefore be paired with integrated land use, transport systems, infrastructure provision, and digital coordination tools.Fourth, governance strategies should be differentiated across regions and development stages. Central China deserves particular attention because it faces stronger emission responsiveness to changes in SCCD. Eastern China is better positioned to explore advanced data-enabled governance models, whereas western China should place greater emphasis on coupling ecological protection with basic public-service improvements and urban functional coordination. Across regions, the priority is to shift from expansion-oriented integration toward efficiency-oriented governance.6.3 Limitations and future researchThis study has several limitations. First, although the SCCD index incorporates digital space into the conventional production-living-ecological framework, the measurement of digital space is constrained by data availability and may not fully capture platform governance, data mobility, algorithmic coordination, or the quality of digital public services. Second, although the empirical design includes fixed effects, robustness checks, and supplementary instrumental-variable estimation, the analysis remains based on observational panel data and cannot eliminate all identification concerns. Third, the mechanism-equation analysis focuses on industrial upgrading and green innovation, but other restructuring pathways--such as energy structure, green finance, or household behavioral change--may also shape emission outcomes.Future research may extend this framework in several directions. More refined measures of digital urban transformation could be introduced, especially those capturing platform governance, data flows, smart service coverage, and the quality of urban digital public goods. Dynamic restructuring deserves greater attention because the emission payoff of industrial upgrading and green innovation may emerge with long lags.
